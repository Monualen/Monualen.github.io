Frequency Division Multiplexing

 Frequency Division Multiplexing (FDM)

The frequency division systems can be divided into 2 categories: 

 SINGLE CHANNEL SYSTEMS

MULTI CHANNEL SYSTEMS

In the first category each channel terminal contains all the electronic devices (oscillators, filters, etc.); while in the second many devices are common to more than one channel.

In the following we will describe the multi channel systems, because they are more important respect to the single channel systems.

The frequency allocation of the phone channels follows the CCITT (European) standard. 
Other allocations are also possible (American standards).

MULTI CHANNEL SYSTEMS

Can be obtained: 

    Allocating, on the same transmitting media, a certain number of  single channel systems in High Frequency;

    Using multi channel systems, constituted by sets of channels like subsystems. 

The first solution is not the better one, because it has many technical and economical difficulties:

    It needs to generate as many carriers as the channels number;

    It requires the use of as many filters as the channels number.

If the channels to be inserted on the same line are greater than a certain number, the direct channel modulation is replaced by a system with successive modulations.

Channel multiplexing

The technique uses basic primary groups. Each group has 12 channels, and each channel has a 4 kHz bandwidth.

The primary groups have a bandwidth of 12·4 = 48 kHz.

The following primary groups have been chosen:

    type A: 12 ÷ 60 kHz

    type B: 60 ÷ 108 kHz

Such a groups can be obtained modulating the single channels with 12 carriers , and filtering the lower side bands                  (see the figure).

 

 

 

This method is not convenient for two main reasons: 

    12 modulators with 12 different carriers are required;

    12 band-pass channel filters are required.

In most of practical applications the "double modulation" is used, which allows a considerable saving of circuits

 

Double Modulation

Double Modulation presents some advantages with respect to Single Modulation:

    Less problems in the practical realization of filters;

    Same carrier frequencies can be used to translate more than one channel

    The number of different parts in a system is reduced. This takes to an improvement of reliability and quality and to a reduction of costs.

Using Double Modulation a type-B Basic Group is assembled as follows:

    12 channels are grouped in three 4-channel groups;

    The 4 channels of each group modulate carriers having frequencies of 8, 12, 16, 20 kHz. Lower sidebands are selected through band-pass filters. Each 4-channel group is thus extending from 8 to 20 kHz; 

    The three 4-channel groups modulate now carriers of 84, 100, 116 kHz. Lower sidebands are still selected and thus the resulting 12-channel FDM system has a bandwidth extending from 60 to 108 kHz..

Only 7 carriers and band-pass filters are used with Double Modulation, instead of 12 as required by Single Modulation technique.

Systems with more than 12 channels can be combined starting from the Basic Group.

Let's consider for example the formation of a 24-channel group.

Two type-B Basic Groups are taken (the type-A Basic Group is not used, since the harmonics of the lowest frequencies fall in the same band 12-60 kHz and can cause disturbances). One Group is transmitted without any further modulation; the other Group modulates a 120 kHz carrier, and then the Lower Side        Band is taken for transmission.

FDM Transmitter & Receiver

The 2-wire voice frequency circuit, coming from the subscriber through the exchange, is connected to the

 hybrid transformer F . It realizes the 2-4 wires transformation in order to allow mono-directional amplifiers and mo-demodulators to be used.

The hybrid, terminated on the impedance LA, connects the voice signal coming from the subscriber to the transmission circuit TX. The signal coming from the remote terminal is sent to the subscriber through the reception circuit RX and the hybrid transformer.

 Transmission section (Tx)

    The attenuator Att.1 regulates the amplitude of the signal coming from the hybrid, in order to deliver a correct level to the modulator Mod.;

    The channel modulator Mod. is a balanced modulator generating the two sidebands. It receives the voice signal (modulating) and the carrier from the carrier generator Gen. and delivers the two sidebands with suppressed carrier;

    The band-pass channel filter F1 selects the desired sideband (upper side band in this case) from the modulated signal. The filter output is connected, in parallel with filters from other channels, to the High Frequency section HF of the terminal equipment;

the HF section gives a basic FDM signal, that may be used to assemble larger groups or may be directly transmitted.

 Reception section (Rx)

    The FDM signal comes from the HF section, where it was amplified and eventually demodulated. HF section includes also an equalizer, which compensates for the frequency response of the transmission link;

    The first block encountered by signal in the channel modem is the channel filter F2. This is a band-pass filter allowing the transit of only frequencies of the desired channel;

    The signal from the channel filter is applied to the demodulator Dem., which delivers the original modulating voice signal. The demodulator uses the same carrier of the modulator;

    The demodulator output is fed to a low-pass filter F3, that eliminates undesired frequencies produced by the demodulation or transmitted for out-of-band signaling (between 0 ÷ 300 Hz or between 3400 ÷ 4000 Hz);

    The amplifier Amp. and the attenuator Att.2 adjust the level of the demodulated voice signal;

    The signal is applied from the attenuator Att.2 to the hybrid transformer F which performs the 4-2 wire transformation. Through the 2-wire circuit the voice signal is connected to the exchange and then to the subscriber.

The simple diagram just examined does not include the signaling circuits and compression/expansion circuits (compandor).

The signaling is normally obtained using an out of band frequency of 3825 Hz; it is applied in TX to the MOD together with the useful band 300 ÷ 3400 Hz, while in reception it is detected with a filter placed after the demodulator.

Compandor

The use of Compandors can produce substantial improvement in the noise and crosstalk performance of a voice channel.

The typical Compandor consists of a volume compressor at the transmitting end of the 4-wire path, before the modulator, and a volume expandor at the receiving end, after the demodulator.

The  COMPRESSOR  acts to  compress the  transmitted volume range, generally by providing gain for low-level signals and less gain, or none, for high-level signals.

Low-level  phonemes, which  convey  most of the actual intelligence, are thus transmitted on the line at higher average level, preventing the line noise from masking them.

The receiving volume EXPANDER performs the reverse function so that the dynamic level response is maintained nearly linear over the system.

The figure illustrates the level modifications produced by the Compandor.

As a simple demonstration of the functioning consider signals A and B and a line noise level C.

The compressor provides high-gain for the low-level signals A and B, and therefore along the line the noise level C is lower than the two signals and does not mask them. At the receiving end the expandor re-establishes the correct dynamic range so that A and B are delivered with their original level. Noise too is subjected to the action of the expandor and is reduced to C'.

Signal to noise ratio is thus improved.

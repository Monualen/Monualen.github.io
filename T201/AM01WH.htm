Power of the modulated signal - Modulation index

The devices used to generate Amplitude Modulation have to introduce a variation in the amplitude of a high frequency signal [carrier] according to the amplitude of a low frequency signal [modulating].

High frequency amplifiers are employed, in which the gain is made dependent on the modulating signal.
Semiconductor devices or tubes can be used according to the required output power.

In AM transmitters we have high level modulation if modulation occurs directly in the final power stage (class C amplifier), low level modulation when it is realized before the final stage (with class A or B amplifiers).

It is an amplifier tuned (through L1 and C7) at the frequency of the HF carrier.

The LF modulating signal is applied to the transistor through the choke CH1.

The LF signal causes a variation in the amplification of the transistor and therefore the output signal contains the amplitude modulation. 

 

 

Amplitude Demodulation

 Diode detector and Distortion of the detected signal

Consider the amplitude modulated carrier of the figure: the modulating signal corresponds to the envelope of the waveform. 

The recovery of the modulating signal Vm  can therefore be carried out through an envelope detector. 

The most used envelope detector is built with a germanium diode and an RC low pass filter.

It works like a half-wave rectifier, since the output voltage follows the positive peaks of the carrier. 
The time constant RC will be chosen in order to get an output signal as close as possible to the amplitude variations of the carrier.

Two types of distortion may arise in diode detectors: 

the detected signal presents a too high ripple if time constant RC is much smaller than the period of the carrier,

if time constant RC is much higher  than the period of the modulating  signal the detected signal doesn't  follow exactly the envelope, but it  will decay exponentially as shown  in the figure.
"Diagonal clipping"  is the name given to this distortion.

  Minimum value of time constant RC (reduction of the ripple)

The first condition to be considered in the choice of time constant RC is the reduction of the ripple on the detected signal. 

The time constant has to be much greater than the period T of the carrier, in order to avoid a too fast decay of the charge of C between the peaks of the modulated carrier: 

 

 

 

 

Figures show examples of signal detected using different values of RC (RC is higher in the lower figure). 

It has to be pointed out that RC cannot be too high, because this would cause «diagonal clipping ».

 

 

Maximum value of time constant RC (diagonal clipping)

A second condition for the choice of the time constant RC can be derived referring to the figure.

If the constant is too high it may happen that, from a certain time t1, the output of the detector does not follow the envelope of the modulated carrier, but decays exponentially according to the discharge of C.

This type of distortion is known as Diagonal Clipping.

The diode is cut-off until the time t2, when the decay reaches the envelope. In fact, during the interval t1-t2 the output is permanently greater than the peaks of the carrier, and the diode is therefore reverse biased.

Detector efficiency and choise of RC

The efficiency of the diode detector is defined as the ratio between the amplitude of the detector output and the amplitude of the input envelope.

We assume that at the carrier frequency F the reactance 1/(2pFC) of the capacitance C is much lower than the resistance R. This is verified if we choose RC >>1/F in order to reduce the ripple.

In this condition the efficiency h depends on the ratio between R and the differential resistance rd of the diode, as plotted in the figure. 

 

 

                                            Final considerations

A high efficiency of the detector is obtained by choosing R much greater than the differential resistance « rd » of the diode [ R/rd > 100 ], 

the time constant RC has to be much greater than period « T » of the carrier in order to minimize the ripple [ RC >> T ] 
« Diagonal clipping » is avoided if RC is lower than a quantity that depends on modulating frequency and modulation index [ RC £ f (w,m) ]. When the modulating signal presents variable amplitude an frequency, the maximum frequency and modulation index have to be considered.

SDB / SSB Modulation

  Single Side Band (SSB) - SSB compared to AM

When a carrier is amplitude modulated by a sine wave, the resulting spectrum consists of three frequencies: carrier and upper and lower side bands. 
The carrier conveys no information, but only an indication of the frequency where the information is located.
Besides that, the two sidebands are images of each other.

It's clear therefore that all the information can be conveyed by using one sideband only. The carrier is superfluous and the other sideband is redundant.
This form of modulation is known as Single Side Band. 

Single Side Band presents the following advantages with respect to Amplitude Modulation:

the bandwidth of SSB is half that of AM; this means for example that a double number of communication channels can be allocated within the same frequency band;

In SSB the total power is reserved for the information (conveyed by a single band); in AM most of the power is given to the carrier.

The most evident disadvantages of SSB with respect to AM are: 

more complex circuits both in modulator and in demodulator;

a local regeneration of the carrier is required in the receiver in order to demodulate the SSB signal

Generation of SSB

SSB is normally generated through a Suppressed Carrier Amplitude Modulation (Double Side Band), followed by the suppression of one of the sidebands.With a proper choice of the frequency carrier and of the suppression filter, the Lower Side Band (LSB) or the Upper Side Band (USB) can be obtained.

Single Side Band is commonly applied in Radio Communications and Frequency Division Multiplexing for telephone transmission systems.

SSB / DDB Demodulation

   Demodulation of SSB

The demodulation of the SSB signal Vm cannot be carried out with the AM envelope detector, since the demodulated signal Vd would result different from the original modulating on

The basic SSB demodulation device is the « product detector », which may be constituted by the «balanced modulator» used for modulation. The received signal (for instance the Upper Side Band F+ f) and the carrier F locally generated in the receiver are applied to the detector. 

The output signal contains many frequency components including the frequency f of the original modulating signal.
All components except f are removed though a low pass filter, so obtaining the correct detected signal.

   Balanced or Chopper modulator

Several types of circuits are used as balanced modulators, as well as different devices (diode, Fet, Mosfet).

The figure shows a typical example of diode balanced modulator.

The carrier VH is fed to the diodes through the terminals A-B.

During positive half waves of VH ,a positive voltage is present between A and B. Diodes D3-D1 and D4-D2 are direct biased and can be considered as practical short circuits. We have a short circuit between D and C as a consequence, and the modulating VL is not fed to the output VM.

During negative half waves of VH diodes are practically open circuits and the modulating signal is fed to the output without modification.

The result of the operations is the already examined waveform of the modulated signal, in which the presence of the modulating signal depends on the carrier.

With respect to balanced modulator, ring modulator delivers sidebands of double amplitude.

Furthermore, the frequency of the modulating signal is not among the components of the modulated output signal. 

The carrier VH is fed to the diodes through the center taps of the two transformers. One pair of diodes (D5-D6 ; D7-D8) is alternately in conduction, so that the modulating signal is delivered to the output directly or reversed.

Phase Lock Loop

 Phase Locked Loop (PLL) - Functioning of the PLL

 

 

 

 

 

The figure shows the function diagram of a basic PLL system.

It contains three basic components:

a phase detector (or phase comparator);

a low pass filter;

a Voltage Controlled Oscillator (VCO).

Without the signal applied to the input, the control voltage vc applied to the VCO is zero. Therefore the VCO generates a frequency  fo called "central frequency" or "free oscillation frequency".

With the signal applied to the input, the phase detector compares the phase of a periodic input signal vi with the phase of the VCO signal vo and provides an signal output ve which is proportional to the phase difference of the two inputs. 

The error voltage ve is then filtered by the loop LP filter and applied to the VCO. The control voltage vc changes the VCO frequency in a direction that reduces the phase difference between the input signal and the VCO signal  vo.

When the loop is locked, the control voltage vc is such that the frequency fo of the VCO is exactly equal to the average frequency fi of the input signal vi . 

It is generally necessary to have a non-zero output from the phase detector to maintain the control voltage needed for lock. 
As a consequence, the loop operates with some phase error present; this error tends to be small in a well-designed loop.

 Typical parameters of PLL - V-f characteristic of PLL

FREE-RUNNING FREQUENCY : fo , frequency of VCO without input signal.

CAPTURE RANGE: That range of frequencies around fo over which the loop will acquire lock with an input signal initially starting out of lock. 
It is primarily controlled by the filter: if |fi - fo| is greater than the filter cut-off frequency the error signal is zero and therefore the VCO will oscillate at its free-running frequency fo .

LOCK RANGE : That range of frequencies in the vicinity of fo over which the VCO, once locked to the input signal, will remain locked. 
Lock-range is greater than Capture-range and depends on the error voltage range of the phase detector and on the frequency range of the VCO.
Once the PLL is locked, the output of the phase detector is a DC voltage and the filter cut-off frequency will have no influence on the system.

PLL Component

VCO : Is an oscillator with an output frequency fo proportional to an input control voltage (vc )

Typical parameters are:

frequency range   :   range of  frequencies generated by the VCO;
linearity : frequency interval over which frequency versus control voltage is linear (w1- w2 in the figure);
conversion gain Ko : Ko = dwo/vc [rad/sec/V], the change of VCO frequency per unit of input voltage. 

The most used oscillators use a varicap diode whose capacity, included in the resonant circuit, changes accordingly to vc.

       LOW PASS FILTER

has the following effects on the system:

1.    Determinates the band where it is possible to lock

2.    Establishes the characteristics of the mesh, regarding to the interference signals. 

3.    Assures a rapid lock of the oscillator to the input signal, when the noise puts the system out of  the lock.

4.    Determinates the characteristics and, in particular, the length of the mesh transient when the input voltage changes suddenly. 

The filter characteristics determinate  the settling time (or pull-in time).

The used filters can be active or passive. The active filters are more used that the passive, because they have better characteristics and are less complex.

PLL Application

The PLL can be used in many applications.

 

 

 

 

 

Typical applications of PLL are:

    Frequency multipliers or dividers

    Frequency synthesizers

    FM demodulators

    Frequency Shift Key demodulators

    Synchronism separators

    Timing recovery

    Carrier regeneration

    Tone detectors

    Speed control of motors 

    Stereo decoders.
